About Potatos


The History of Potatos


Potatos were domesticated over 7,000 years ago by the Inca Empire. They were used for eating, relgious ceremonies, and a measurement of time (how much time it took to cook).

Potatos were taken to Europe by Spanish Conquistadors in the 15th century. Initially, people were scared of potatos because they looked like a similar poisonous plant.

However, because potatos could grow in bad soil conditions and their high calorie content, these fears were overcome, and potatos soon became an important part of the diet of many europeans.

The reliance on potatos is exemplified in the Irish Potato Famine (1845-49), when a disease destroyed almost all of the potatos in Ireland.

In 6 years, 1 million people (about 13 percent of the population before) died directly because of the lack of potatos.


Potato Farming Today


Potatos in Washington are usually planted in the months of Febuary-May.

Potatos today are grown with "seed potatos" (special potatos with "eyes" that can grow the plants) in fields of around 100 acres.

These seed potatos are loaded into a specialized potato planter towed by a tractor.

These potatos are watered by a sprinkler system that moves in a circle around a point, known as a center pivot:


Image source:"Idaho Statesman"


To minimize diseases, potato farmers usually use a potato plot every 4 years, giving viruses time to die out before replanting.

After the potatos have grown, farmers usually "kill" their fields by stopping the water supply 10 days before harvest.

Killing the fields makes the potatos grow a thicker skin, allowing them to be harvested without damage.

Lastly, a specialized potato harvester harvests the potatos, seperating out rocks and other forign materials.

These potatos are either immediately sold or stored in specialized storage buildings for up to 11 months.
